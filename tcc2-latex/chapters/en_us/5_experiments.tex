\subsection{Write Performance}
\label{sec:write-results}

\subsubsection{Baseline Sequential Ingestion}

Table~\ref{tab:write-baseline} shows baseline write performance at small scale under the tuned
configuration. IoTDB achieves 2.16\,M\,pts/s, approximately 5$\times$ InfluxDB and 12$\times$
TimescaleDB.

\begin{table*}[t]
  \centering
  \caption{Baseline write performance at small scale (tuned). Throughput in pts/s.}
  \label{tab:write-baseline}
  \begin{tabular}{lrrrr}
    \hline
    \textbf{DB} & \textbf{pts/s} & \textbf{Avg\,Lat} & \textbf{P99\,Lat} & \textbf{Avg\,CPU} \\
    \hline
    IoTDB       & 2{,}163{,}809 &  1.73\,ms &   1.18\,ms &  5.1\% \\
    InfluxDB    &   397{,}894   & 12.00\,ms & 190.50\,ms &  8.5\% \\
    TimescaleDB &   174{,}787   & 27.70\,ms & 223.00\,ms & 22.5\% \\
    \hline
  \end{tabular}
\end{table*}

Three compounding architectural factors explain the gap.

\textbf{Protocol depth.} IoTDB's SESSION\_BY\_TABLET protocol submits a typed columnar
tablet directly over a persistent TCP connection using binary Thrift serialization. One
syscall submits a full batch directly into the TsFile write buffer. InfluxDB requires an
HTTP request per write, including connection management, line-protocol text parsing, and
TSI index lookup. TimescaleDB routes every write through the full PostgreSQL stack:
TCP, SQL parsing, query planning, hypertable routing, WAL write, and MVCC tuple formatting.
Each layer adds latency; the PostgreSQL stack adds the most.

\textbf{Memory buffering and durability trade-offs.} IoTDB accumulates tablets in its
JVM heap and acknowledges writes before disk persistence, flushing to TsFile asynchronously
when the memtable fills. This explicitly trades durability guarantee for throughput, a
trade-off that must be evaluated per deployment. InfluxDB's TSM cache operates similarly
but is bounded (8\,GB in the tuned configuration). TimescaleDB follows standard PostgreSQL
semantics: every write is flushed through the WAL before acknowledgement, providing the
strongest durability guarantee at the cost of per-write synchronous I/O.

\textbf{Index maintenance.} IoTDB's write path is thin above its JVM buffer. InfluxDB's
TSM engine maintains a Time Series Index (TSI) on every write. TimescaleDB's B-tree and
BRIN indexes grow with data volume, and autovacuum activity increases write overhead
as the hypertable expands.

The combination of these three factors indicates that IoTDB's write advantage has an
architectural basis: the binary protocol, asynchronous buffering, and absence of
synchronous index maintenance act cumulatively and are not eliminated by configuration
tuning.

Table~\ref{tab:batch} shows how throughput changes between BATCH\_SIZE=1 (one write request
per point) and BATCH\_SIZE=1000 (1,000 points per request).

\begin{table}[ht]
  \centering
  \caption{Batch size sensitivity at small scale (tuned). Throughput in pts/s.}
  \label{tab:batch}
  \begin{tabular}{lrrr}
    \hline
    \textbf{DB} & \textbf{BATCH=1} & \textbf{BATCH=1000} & \textbf{Ratio} \\
    \hline
    IoTDB       &  28{,}593 & 11{,}827{,}572 & 413$\times$ \\
    InfluxDB    &   5{,}024 &  2{,}667{,}256 & 531$\times$ \\
    TimescaleDB &   5{,}120 &    214{,}927   &  42$\times$ \\
    \hline
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

IoTDB and InfluxDB show 413--531$\times$ speedup, reflecting the high cost of per-request
overhead. For InfluxDB, this is an HTTP round-trip plus TCP connection overhead; for IoTDB,
it is the fixed cost of a Thrift call per tablet. TimescaleDB's 42$\times$ ratio is
markedly lower because PostgreSQL's WAL flush already dominates at BATCH=1: the per-row
overhead is so high that larger batches provide proportionally less benefit; the bottleneck
shifts from protocol overhead to storage I/O.

This result has direct practical significance. An IoT application that sends one sensor
reading per HTTP request to InfluxDB will achieve 531$\times$ less throughput than one
that buffers locally and writes in batches. At any write volume where batching is feasible,
it is the single most impactful configuration choice for InfluxDB and IoTDB users.

TimescaleDB shows the smallest relative degradation because PostgreSQL's B-tree index

needed to accommodate out-of-sequence points.

In absolute throughput, IoTDB still far leads under out-of-order conditions (1.87\,M vs.\
255\,K pts/s for InfluxDB). Two dimensions must therefore be considered separately: absolute
out-of-order throughput (IoTDB leads at every scale) and relative degradation from write
disorder (TimescaleDB is most resilient with the smallest fractional penalty).

\subsection{Read Performance}
\label{sec:read-results}

An important note on interpreting read throughput: the pts/s figure for a read test sums
the point counts returned by all active query types. A range query returning hundreds of
points per call will appear to have far higher pts/s than a latest-point query that returns
exactly one point per call. These figures are therefore not comparable across read test types.
\textbf{Latency} is used exclusively as the primary read comparison metric in this section.

A methodological note relevant to all read comparisons: read tests run sequentially on the
same dataset within a session, so later tests benefit from warmer OS page caches and
in-memory database structures (see Section~\ref{sec:limitations}). Where warm-cache effects
are the stated mechanism for a database's performance, such as TimescaleDB's
\texttt{shared\_buffers} for downsampling, this is an expected and reproducible property
under normal operating conditions, not an artefact.

\subsubsection{Latest-Point Queries}

Table~\ref{tab:latest} shows latest-point average latency across all three scales for the
tuned runs.

\begin{table}[ht]
  \centering
  \caption{Latest-point average latency (ms) across scales (tuned). Lower is better.}
  \label{tab:latest}
  \begin{tabular}{lrrr}
    \hline
    \textbf{DB} & \textbf{Small} & \textbf{Medium} & \textbf{Large} \\
    \hline
    TimescaleDB &  0.38 &  0.41 &  0.62 \\
    IoTDB       &  1.24 &  0.51 &  0.68 \\
    InfluxDB    &  9.24 & 21.72 & 23.24 \\
    \hline
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

TimescaleDB and IoTDB maintain sub-millisecond or near-sub-millisecond latency across
a hundredfold increase in data volume. Both exploit database-specific shortcuts for last-value retrieval.
TimescaleDB executes a \texttt{SELECT~...~ORDER~BY~time~DESC~LIMIT~1} against the most
recent hypertable chunk via a reverse B-tree scan; because hypertable chunks are ordered
by time, this always touches the smallest possible amount of data. IoTDB maintains a
dedicated in-memory last-value cache per series, serving the query entirely from RAM
without accessing TsFile at all.

InfluxDB has no equivalent shortcut. It must seek to the tail of each series' TSM file
blocks, and that seek cost grows as more TSM files accumulate over the dataset lifetime.
The resulting progression (9.24\,ms at small, 21.72\,ms at medium, 23.24\,ms at
large) confirms an approximately linear relationship between stored data and latest-point
cost. Across the evaluated scales, InfluxDB's latest-point latency showed approximately linear
growth with stored data volume. In deployments without active retention or downsampling
policies, this pattern may make it disadvantageous for high-frequency real-time
monitoring; deployments that apply such policies may mitigate this behavior.

Table~\ref{tab:downsample} shows GROUP~BY time-bucket downsampling latency.

\begin{table}[ht]

  \label{tab:downsample}
  \begin{tabular}{lrrr}
    \hline
    \textbf{DB} & \textbf{Small} & \textbf{Medium} & \textbf{Large} \\
    \hline
    TimescaleDB & 0.61 & 0.52 & 0.73 \\
    IoTDB       & 1.81 & 0.64 & 0.95 \\
    InfluxDB    & 5.71 & 5.65 & 9.22 \\
    \hline
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

TimescaleDB's \texttt{time\_bucket()} aggregation stays below 1\,ms across a hundredfold
increase in data volume, a result that holds consistently across all tested scales. The key is that \texttt{time\_bucket()}
queries scan a fixed time window rather than the full dataset: once the relevant chunks
are warm in \texttt{shared\_buffers}, query cost is bounded by the window size, not by
total stored data. This property makes TimescaleDB an excellent fit for dashboard workloads
that consistently query recent time windows regardless of historical data accumulation.

IoTDB's downsampling latency improves from small to medium scale (1.81\,ms $\to$ 0.64\,ms)
as the longer medium-scale run gives the JVM JIT compiler time to optimise the chunk
aggregation hot path. At large scale it remains under 1\,ms. InfluxDB's Flux aggregation
engine incurs higher per-query overhead (5--9\,ms) from expression evaluation and operator
pipeline costs.

\subsubsection{Value-Filter Queries}

Value-filter queries (predicates on sensor values such as \texttt{temperature > 85.0})
are the most architecturally discriminating workload in this study. Time-based indexes
cannot answer them directly; each engine must either scan all stored values or exploit
a secondary data structure to prune non-matching storage blocks.

Table~\ref{tab:vfilter} shows value-filter latency and its scaling behavior across scales.

\begin{table*}[t]
  \centering
  \caption{Value-filter average latency (ms) across scales (tuned).
  The ratio column shows latency growth from small to large scale (data volume one hundred times larger).}
  \label{tab:vfilter}
  \begin{tabular}{lrrrr}
    \hline
    \textbf{DB} & \textbf{Small} & \textbf{Medium} & \textbf{Large} & \textbf{S$\to$L ratio} \\
    \hline
    IoTDB       &   3.72 &    9.80 &   19.42 &  5.2$\times$ \\
    TimescaleDB &  23.98 &  130.08 &  510.05 & 21.3$\times$ \\
    InfluxDB    &  71.09 &  310.75 &  496.81 &  7.0$\times$ \\
    \hline
  \end{tabular}
\end{table*}

IoTDB's advantage is large and \textit{improves} with scale. Each TsFile chunk stores
per-series min/max statistics. A value predicate can skip entire chunks whose stored range
does not intersect the predicate threshold, providing lightweight secondary indexing at
the storage layer. At large scale, with more data per chunk, a larger fraction of chunks
can be pruned, producing the sublinear scaling ratio (5.2$\times$ for a hundredfold increase in data).
This is the expected behavior of a statistics-based skip structure: the pruning ratio
improves as chunks grow denser.

TimescaleDB uses PostgreSQL's BRIN (Block Range INdex), which records min/max values per
block range on disk. BRIN provides coarser-grained pruning than IoTDB's per-chunk statistics
and degrades at a superlinear rate (21.3$\times$ for a hundredfold increase in data). At large scale,
TimescaleDB averages 510\,ms per value-filter query, a 26$\times$ disadvantage relative
to IoTDB. This workload also drives TimescaleDB to its highest CPU utilisation (77\% average,
83\% peak), indicating the query executor is the bottleneck.

InfluxDB has no statistics-based pruning structure but benefits from some coarse file-level
skipping based on time-range metadata in the TSM index, producing a 7$\times$ scaling ratio,
significantly better than TimescaleDB's but still 10$\times$ worse than IoTDB in absolute
terms at large scale.

\subsubsection{Range Queries}

Range queries retrieve all data points within a time window without value filtering.
TimescaleDB is fastest here: 0.54\,ms at small scale and 0.66\,ms at large, benefiting
from hypertable chunk pruning (only chunks overlapping the query window are opened) and the
warm OS page cache that avoids disk I/O for frequently queried recent time windows. IoTDB
achieves 1.19--1.21\,ms and is effectively scale-insensitive within this data range.
InfluxDB achieves 5.41--8.68\,ms, where the TSM block scanner bears higher per-file
overhead than TimescaleDB's chunk-level sequential scan.

\subsection{Memory Footprint}
\label{sec:memory-results}

Memory measurements require careful interpretation because the original shared-start
benchmark runs produced substantially inflated figures through a methodological artefact.

\textbf{TimescaleDB} reported approximately 5.4\,GB from the very first test in the
original runs. This was not PostgreSQL's buffer pool usage; it was accumulated OS page
cache from IoTDB's preceding write sessions. Linux accounts for file-system page cache
toward a container's RSS in \texttt{docker stats}, and the idle TimescaleDB container
had been running for hours while IoTDB generated gigabytes of I/O. In the tuned
sequential runs, TimescaleDB starts at 239\,MB and grows to approximately 1.8\,GB
after the write tests warm \texttt{shared\_buffers}. The 1.8\,GB figure is the
accurate operational footprint for this dataset size under the tuned configuration.

\textbf{IoTDB} reported approximately 12.5\,GB flat throughout the original runs.
During the hours it spent idle while InfluxDB and TimescaleDB were tested, its JVM
speculatively pre-allocated the configured maximum heap in anticipation of future load.
In the tuned runs the JVM starts at 1.7\,GB and grows organically to approximately
10\,GB by the end of the read phase, reflecting actual working-set demand per workload
phase rather than speculative reservation.

\textbf{InfluxDB} is the most memory-efficient across write workloads: 190--940\,MB in
tuned write tests at small scale. However, this advantage does not hold unconditionally.
At large scale, BATCH-LARGE peaks at 2.9\,GB as the TSM write cache holds large volumes
of pending batches alongside concurrent compaction buffers. The commonly cited claim that
InfluxDB's memory is ``capped at 1\,GB by default'' describes only the TSM cache ceiling;
total process RSS includes the series index, WAL buffers, and Go runtime overhead that
together can substantially exceed that limit under high write load.

The practical implication for deployment planning is that IoTDB's dominant write throughput
requires a substantial memory commitment ($\sim$10\,GB at medium scale, growing to $\sim$15\,GB
at large). For memory-constrained environments (IoT edge gateways, embedded systems,
or cloud instances with limited RAM), InfluxDB's leaner footprint may be the deciding
factor even where write throughput is the primary concern.

\subsubsection{IoTDB BATCH-SMALL JIT Saturation}

IoTDB's \texttt{batch-small} throughput across scales (28\,K $\to$ 81\,K $\to$ 446\,K pts/s)
is one of the clearest demonstrations of JIT-driven scaling in the dataset. Average latency
simultaneously \textit{decreases} from 1.22\,ms to 0.40\,ms as scale increases, which is
only possible if the improvement comes from code optimization rather than reduced load.
The large-scale figure (446\,K pts/s, 0.40\,ms average latency) represents IoTDB's
steady-state single-point ingestion throughput once the JIT has fully compiled the
Thrift serialisation hot path.

\subsubsection{TimescaleDB BATCH-LARGE Timeout}

TimescaleDB's \texttt{batch-large} test hits the 3{,}600-second benchmark timeout at large
scale in both the original and tuned runs. With 50 devices and a batch size of 1{,}000 rows,
each write request touches 50 hypertable partitions simultaneously and triggers up to 50
concurrent WAL flushes. The cumulative I/O cost exhausts the time budget before the full
loop count completes. This is not a pathological edge case but a foreseeable consequence of
hypertable partition fan-out under large-batch high-concurrency writes. For bulk-loading
workloads targeting TimescaleDB at scale, the partition count must be weighed against the
WAL flush amplification.

\subsubsection{InfluxDB Latest-Point Linear Growth}

Among the multi-scale trends, InfluxDB's latest-point latency progression (9.24 $\to$
21.72 $\to$ 23.24\,ms) deserves particular attention because it reveals a structural
scalability issue. Every incremental TSM file written to disk adds to the seek depth
that a latest-point query must traverse. TimescaleDB and IoTDB show flat or even
\textit{decreasing} latest-point latency (due to JIT warm-up for IoTDB) across the
same hundredfold increase in data volume. Any deployment that will accumulate months or years of
data will see InfluxDB's latest-point cost continue to grow, while TimescaleDB's
and IoTDB's costs remain bounded.

The primary driver is \texttt{shared\_buffers=8GB}, which provides PostgreSQL a large
enough buffer pool to avoid buffer evictions that force extra WAL writes.
\texttt{work\_mem=27413kB} eliminates disk spills for sort-based predicate evaluation,

latency (BATCH-LARGE P99 dropped from 1{,}069\,ms to 812\,ms). These improvements
indicate that default PostgreSQL settings are intentionally conservative to ensure
correctness on low-resource hardware, and that tuning via pgtune or an equivalent tool
should be applied before reporting results on mid- to high-end hardware.

\subsubsection{InfluxDB Gains from Isolation}

InfluxDB received no configuration changes in the tuned runs. Its improvements come entirely
from running without competing databases. All read latencies improved 15--21\%, consistent
with better TSM file cache utilization once TimescaleDB's page cache no longer competes for
OS buffer space. The most dramatic result was BATCH-LARGE P99: 205\,ms $\to$ 60\,ms
($-$71\%). In the original setup, TimescaleDB was writing aggressively to disk during
InfluxDB's test session, periodically starving InfluxDB's TSM compaction engine of I/O
bandwidth. These rare but severe I/O stalls produced extreme latency outliers that did not
affect average throughput but inflated P99 dramatically. Isolation removed these stalls.

\subsubsection{Correcting Two Large-Scale False Positives}

The original large-scale runs produced two findings that the tuned reruns reversed.

\textbf{TimescaleDB out-of-order penalty.} The original large-scale run showed a 23\%
OOO throughput penalty for TimescaleDB, attributed to decompression-recompression cycles
from out-of-order inserts into older compressed chunks. The tuned rerun shows only 2.7\%
degradation, consistent with the pattern at small and medium scale. The mechanism:
out-of-order inserts into older chunks require reading those chunks from disk to decompress
them. In the original shared-start session, IoTDB's expanding JVM heap (15+ GB) had evicted
the OS page cache pages holding those chunks, forcing cold disk reads for every OOO insert
into an older chunk. With exclusive access, the chunks remain warm in cache and decompression
costs are negligible.

\textbf{TimescaleDB CPU saturation.} The original READ workload at large scale averaged
97.7\% CPU utilization with a P99 latency of 4{,}232\,ms, leading to the conclusion that
TimescaleDB ``runs out of compute'' and could not be improved without a stronger CPU or
horizontal sharding. The tuned rerun shows 77.4\% average CPU and 1{,}850\,ms P99.
The original 97.7\% figure was the result of all three databases and the Java benchmark
client simultaneously competing for the same six CPU cores. TimescaleDB was not at a
hardware ceiling: it was CPU-starved by competing processes.

These two reversals underscore the importance of isolated, correctly-tuned benchmark
methodology. Without sequential container lifecycles and standard database configuration,
both claims would have been published as validated findings.

\subsection{Mixed Read Workload}

The \texttt{read} workload runs all ten iot-benchmark query types with equal weight,
providing a composite read baseline that includes range queries, aggregations, downsampling,
latest-point lookups, and value-predicate queries simultaneously. At small scale (tuned),
TimescaleDB averages 0.67\,ms, IoTDB averages 2.11\,ms, and InfluxDB averages 14.28\,ms
per operation across the combined set. These figures are consistent with the individual
query results presented above: TimescaleDB dominates for time-windowed queries, which
constitute the majority of the ten types, while IoTDB's advantage on value-filter queries
is diluted by the equal weighting.

The mixed workload's primary analytical role in this study is as a tuning benchmark
(Table~\ref{tab:tsdb-tuning}): TimescaleDB's READ latency improved 38\% after pgtune,
confirming that multiple query types, not only value-filter, benefit from a correctly
sized buffer pool.

\subsection{Summary}
\label{sec:results-summary}

Table~\ref{tab:summary} consolidates the workload-winner mapping across all tested scenarios.

\begin{table*}[t]
  \centering
  \caption{Workload recommendation summary across all tested scenarios.}
  \footnotesize
  \label{tab:summary}
  \begin{tabular}{l l p{0.55\linewidth}}
    \hline
    \textbf{Workload} & \textbf{Best DB} & \textbf{Key architectural reason} \\
    \hline
    Sequential ingestion (ordered)     & IoTDB       & Binary tablet protocol + in-memory buffering before TsFile flush \\
    Bulk loading (BATCH=1000)          & IoTDB       & Columnar vectorised write path; 11.8\,M pts/s at small scale \\
    Single-point ingestion (BATCH=1)   & IoTDB       & Lower per-call Thrift overhead vs HTTP round-trip \\
    Out-of-order (absolute throughput) & IoTDB       & 1.87\,M pts/s OOO vs 255\,K (InfluxDB); leads at every scale \\
    Out-of-order (relative tolerance)  & TimescaleDB & B-tree updates are order-independent \\
    Dashboard downsampling             & TimescaleDB & \texttt{time\_bucket()} in hot \texttt{shared\_buffers}; constant latency \\
    Bulk time-range export             & TimescaleDB & Chunk pruning + warm OS page cache \\
    Threshold alerting (value-filter)  & IoTDB       & Chunk min/max statistics; sublinear scaling with data volume \\
    Real-time last-value lookup        & TimescaleDB & Sub-ms reverse B-tree scan on most recent chunk \\
    Memory efficiency (write)          & InfluxDB    & Bounded TSM cache; no JVM pre-allocation \\
    \hline
  \end{tabular}
\end{table*}

IoTDB is the clear choice for write-dominated workloads but requires substantial memory
($\sim$10\,GB at medium scale, growing to $\sim$15\,GB at large) and does not support
relational joins. TimescaleDB trades raw write speed for SQL compatibility, the full
PostgreSQL ecosystem, and consistently strong read performance for time-windowed queries
once data is cached. InfluxDB occupies the middle ground: better write throughput than
TimescaleDB, better memory efficiency than IoTDB, but weaker on complex value-predicate
queries and latest-value lookups at scale. No single database dominates across all workloads.
